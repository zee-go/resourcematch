## The True Cost of Hiring a Filipino Accountant: A Full Stack View

Most outsourcing cost comparisons are built around base salary. That's a useful starting point, but it's incomplete. To make a defensible financial case — internally or to a board — you need the **fully loaded cost** of each hire. That means salary plus every recurring and one-time expense attached to it.

Here's how a senior Filipino accounting professional compares to a US-based equivalent when you account for the full picture.

### Base Salary: Where the Gap Starts

A **senior US-based accountant** with 5–10 years of experience and CPA credentials commands a base salary of **$75,000–$110,000 per year**, depending on location and specialization. In high-cost metros like New York, San Francisco, or Chicago, that floor is closer to $90,000. A senior financial controller or accounting manager clears $120,000+ without breaking a sweat.

A **senior Filipino CPA or accounting specialist** with equivalent experience — GAAP-proficient, experienced in QuickBooks, Xero, NetSuite, or SAP, and capable of owning a full accounting function — earns between **$18,000–$36,000 per year** (approximately PHP 85,000–160,000/month at current exchange rates). The specific range depends on the complexity of the role, industry specialization, and the individual's track record.

For context:
- **Junior Filipino accountant (2–4 years):** $12,000–$18,000/year
- **Mid-level Filipino accountant (4–7 years):** $16,000–$26,000/year
- **Senior Filipino CPA or Controller (7–12+ years):** $24,000–$36,000/year
- **Finance Manager or VP-level equivalent:** $36,000–$55,000/year

The salary gap alone is significant. But it gets wider when you add benefits.

### Benefits and Mandatory Contributions

In the US, a standard benefits package — health insurance, dental, vision, 401(k) match, PTO payout liability — typically adds **20–35% on top of base salary**. For a $90,000/year accountant, that's an additional **$18,000–$31,500 per year**, bringing your true employment cost to **$108,000–$121,500** before you've bought a single piece of software or paid for their desk.

For a remote Filipino professional, the picture is different. The Philippines has mandatory government contributions (SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG) that are relatively modest — combined employer contributions run roughly **$800–$1,500/year** for a full-time employee at this salary level. Many companies also provide a **13th-month pay** (required by Philippine law), which adds one month's salary annually — so budget that in.

A fair, competitive package for a senior Filipino accounting hire looks like this:
- **Annual salary:** $24,000–$36,000
- **13th month pay:** $2,000–$3,000
- **Mandatory government contributions (employer share):** $800–$1,500
- **HMO health coverage (private, common among employers):** $500–$1,200/year
- **Internet allowance or equipment stipend:** $500–$1,000 one-time or annual

**Total annual cost: approximately $28,000–$42,700**

Compared to the US equivalent at $108,000–$121,500, you're looking at a **65–75% cost reduction** on a genuinely senior, credentialed hire.

### Overhead, Office, and Infrastructure

This is where remote hiring compounds the savings in ways that don't fit neatly into a salary comparison.

A US-based employee requires physical or subsidized workspace. Even with hybrid arrangements, there's equipment provisioning, ergonomic compliance, IT support overhead, HR administration time, and in some states, specific labor law compliance infrastructure. The Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM) estimates the **fully loaded cost of a US employee is 1.25–1.4x their base salary** — and that's before accounting for office space at $10,000–$20,000/year per desk in most US markets.

A remote Filipino professional works from their own space. Your infrastructure cost is:
- **Laptop or equipment:** $800–$1,500 (often a one-time employer contribution)
- **Accounting software seat:** $50–$300/month depending on platform
- **Communication tools:** Already part of your existing stack
- **Payroll processing:** $50–$150/month via Deel, Remote.com, or a local EOR

You are not paying for their commute, their parking, their office snacks, or their building's utilities. These are not trivial numbers at scale.

## Recruitment, Onboarding, and Turnover Costs

The salary comparison is the headline. But the **cost of a bad hire** — or a slow hire — is often where companies actually lose money.

### US Recruitment Costs

Hiring a senior US accountant through a staffing agency typically costs **15–25% of first-year salary** in placement fees. For a $90,000 hire, that's $13,500–$22,500 upfront. Even direct hiring through job boards (LinkedIn, Indeed) runs $3,000–$8,000 in ad spend, recruiter time, and interview cycles that take 6–10 weeks on average.

Turnover in US accounting roles runs high. The Bureau of Labor Statistics consistently shows accounting and finance roles have **annual voluntary turnover rates of 15–20%** in competitive markets. When a senior accountant leaves, you absorb:
- Recruitment fees again
- 30–90 days of productivity loss during transition
- Knowledge transfer gaps that can distort your books for a quarter or more

The **cost of replacing a senior US accountant is routinely estimated at 50–200% of their annual salary** when all factors are accounted for.

## Time Zone and Workflow Integration

One objection that comes up regularly: "Does the time zone difference cause problems?"

In practice, it depends entirely on how you structure the role. The Philippines is **UTC+8**, which means:

- **For US East Coast companies:** overlap hours are available early morning (6–9am Manila time = 5–8pm EST the prior evening). Many senior Filipino professionals working with US clients shift their hours to **10am–7pm Manila time**, giving 3–4 hours of real-time overlap with US East Coast business hours.
- **For US West Coast companies:** the overlap is more natural — a Manila professional working 2pm–11pm local time has a full business day aligned with PST.
- **For EU companies:** the Philippines time zone overlaps well with early-afternoon EU hours.

For accounting roles specifically, **asynchronous workflows are the norm**. Month-end close, reconciliation, reporting, and audit prep are not real-time activities. What matters is reliability, output quality, and clear communication — all of which are structurally independent of time zone.

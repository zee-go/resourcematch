# US Guide: Time Zones & Async Work with Filipino Teams

The biggest operational question US companies ask before hiring Filipino professionals is not about skills or salary. It is: *how does the time zone actually work in practice?* Working with a Filipino remote team from the US means navigating a 12 to 16-hour time difference, depending on your location and the time of year. Done without a plan, this gap creates delays and frustration. Done correctly, it creates a near-24-hour operating cycle that makes your business faster, not slower.

This guide gives you a specific, field-tested framework for scheduling, async handoffs, and overlap windows, built around the realities of the Philippines-US time zone difference.

---

## Understanding the Philippines-US Time Zone Difference

Manila operates on Philippine Standard Time (PST), which is UTC+8. There is no daylight saving adjustment in the Philippines, which actually simplifies scheduling once you account for it.

Here is what the gap looks like across US time zones:

| US Time Zone | Offset from Manila (standard) | 9 AM Manila = US time |
|---|---|---|
| Eastern (EST) | 13 hours behind | 8 PM previous day |
| Central (CST) | 14 hours behind | 7 PM previous day |
| Mountain (MST) | 15 hours behind | 6 PM previous day |
| Pacific (PST) | 16 hours behind | 5 PM previous day |

The practical implication: your Filipino team's standard workday (9 AM to 6 PM Manila) runs entirely during your previous evening. There is no natural same-day overlap without one side adjusting their schedule.

This is not a dealbreaker. It is a design constraint, and constraints clarify good system design.

---

## The Two Overlap Strategies That Actually Work

Most US companies use one of two approaches, depending on role requirements.

### Strategy 1: Shifted Hours with a Targeted Overlap Window

For roles where live collaboration matters, such as an operations manager coordinating with a US logistics team, a 2 to 3-hour overlap window is usually sufficient. Ask your Filipino professional to shift their workday to start at 2 PM or 3 PM Manila time. This creates an overlap that lands in US morning hours.

For example, a Filipino operations manager working 2 PM to 11 PM Manila can have a live sync with a New York team from 1 AM to 2 AM Manila (which is 1 PM to 2 PM Eastern). That is one clean overlap hour without requiring either party to work unreasonable hours.

For Pacific-based companies, the math is more favorable. A 1 PM Manila start lands a Filipino professional's afternoon squarely in Pacific morning hours, giving you 3 to 4 hours of real-time availability.

**Key point:** Filipino senior professionals in roles like [operations management](/blog/how-to-hire-filipino-operations-manager) are accustomed to shifted schedules. It should be disclosed upfront in the offer, and compensated with a shift differential when the adjustment is significant.

### Strategy 2: Pure Async with Structured Handoffs

For finance and accounting roles, synchronous overlap is rarely necessary for day-to-day work. A senior Filipino accountant reconciling books in QuickBooks or reviewing AP/AR in NetSuite does not need to be on a call with your US team to do excellent work. What they need is a reliable handoff protocol.

The async model works like this:

- **End-of-day handoff (US side):** Before the US team signs off, they document open items, flag urgent priorities, and drop any relevant files into a shared workspace (Notion, ClickUp, Slack, or equivalent).
- **Overnight execution (Manila side):** The Filipino professional works through those tasks during their standard Manila daytime, completes the work, and logs a status update with outputs.
- **Morning review (US side):** The US team arrives to finished work, reviewed reports, or resolved exceptions. Questions get answered asynchronously before Manila's next workday begins.

Done well, this creates a daily cycle that feels like having a night shift without the overhead of managing one.

## Building an Async Workflow System That Holds Up

Ad-hoc async fails. Structured async scales. The difference is documentation discipline on both sides.

### The Daily Handoff Template

Every US-side handoff should include four things:

1. **What was completed today** (context for the Filipino team picking up)
2. **What needs action tonight** (specific tasks, not vague requests)
3. **Priority level and deadline** (is this needed by 9 AM Eastern or end of week?)
4. **Where the relevant files or data live** (no hunting)

This sounds simple. Most teams skip step one and four, and then wonder why they get incomplete outputs.

### Tool Stack Recommendations for US-Philippines Teams

For **finance and accounting workflows:** QuickBooks Online or NetSuite for the core ledger, Bill.com for AP/AR workflows, Loom for async video reviews of reports, and a shared Notion or Google Workspace for SOPs.

For **operations workflows:** ClickUp or Asana for task management with due-date visibility, Slack with clear channel conventions (no urgent pings outside agreed windows), and Loom again for walking through process changes.

**One Slack convention worth adopting immediately:** create a dedicated channel for cross-timezone handoffs only. Keep it clean. No chatter, only structured status drops. This prevents important handoffs from getting buried in general conversation.

### Setting Response Time Expectations

The most common friction point in US-Philippines async is unclear response expectations. Define these explicitly during onboarding:

- **Urgent items** (flagged in the handoff): response or completion before US team's start of next business day
- **Standard items**: completed within the same Manila working cycle
- **Low-priority or FYI items**: acknowledged within 24 hours

This is not micromanagement. It is a shared operating agreement, and senior Filipino professionals expect and appreciate this kind of clarity.

## Compliance and Contractor Classification Notes

If you are engaging Filipino professionals as independent contractors rather than through an employer of record, you are required to collect a **W-8BEN form** (not a W-9, which is for US persons). This establishes their non-US status for IRS purposes and determines withholding treatment.

Filipino contractors are generally not subject to US self-employment tax, but you should confirm this with your tax advisor based on the nature of the engagement. For companies with more complex structures or SOX compliance requirements, ensure your Filipino finance professionals understand your internal controls framework from day one. ## Key Takeaways

- The Philippines-US time gap is 12 to 16 hours depending on your time zone. Treat it as a design constraint, not a problem.
- Shifted hours (2 PM to 3 PM Manila start) work well for roles requiring live collaboration. Pure async works well for most finance and accounting functions.
- Structured handoffs with four clear components (completed work, action items, priorities, file locations) prevent the majority of async breakdowns.
- Use W-8BEN, not W-9, for Filipino independent contractors. Consult your tax advisor on withholding treatment.
- Senior professionals adapt to these systems quickly. The limiting factor is almost always the US side's documentation habits, not the offshore team's capabilities.
